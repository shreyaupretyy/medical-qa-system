**Sepsis Management Guideline**

**Category:** Critical Care

### 1. Definition and Epidemiology

Sepsis is a life-threatening condition caused by an uncontrolled infection leading to organ dysfunction. The source material does not provide prevalence or incidence rates, but it highlights the severity of sepsis as a complication of abortion.

Risk factors for sepsis include:

* History of criminal interference with pregnancy
* Presence of infected retained products of conception

### 2. Clinical Presentation

The clinical presentation of sepsis includes:

* Severe lower abdominal pain
* Fever (temperature may be normal)
* Vomiting
* Headache
* Offensive, bloody vaginal discharge
* Signs of shock: tachycardia, pulse > 90 bpm, hypotension (systolic BP < 90 mmHg), peritonism, bulky tender uterus

### 3. Diagnostic Criteria and Workup

Diagnostic criteria for sepsis include:

* Presence of infection (e.g., infected retained products of conception)
* Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) criteria:
	+ Temperature > 38°C or < 36°C
	+ Heart rate > 90 bpm
	+ Respiratory rate > 20 breaths/min or PaCO2 < 32 mmHg
	+ White blood cell count > 12,000 cells/μL or < 4,000 cells/μL

Laboratory tests:

* Complete Blood Count (CBC)
* Clotting screen
* Blood culture and sensitivity
* Urine culture and sensitivity

Imaging studies:

* Abdomino-pelvic ultrasound scan
* Plain X-ray of abdomen

### 4. Treatment Algorithm

**First-line treatment:**

1. Insert the largest bore cannula in the largest vein visible.
2. Raise drip stand or squeeze bag to increase infusion rate.
3. Give colloids (e.g., Ringers lactate).
4. In hemorrhagic states, cross-matched blood is preferred; resuscitate with crystalloids (e.g., normal saline) in the meantime.

**Second-line options:**

1. Furosemide IV for re-establishing diuresis after adequate fluid replacement.
2. Blood transfusion (in severe bleeding).
3. Plasma replacement (in cases of severe burns).

**Treatment modifications based on severity:**

* In septic shock, insert two cannulae at separate sites for rapid IV infusion.

### 5. Management and Follow-up

Monitoring parameters:

* Vital signs
* Urine output
* Electrolyte levels
* Blood gas analysis

Follow-up schedule:

* Daily monitoring of vital signs and laboratory tests.
* Adjust treatment plan based on response to therapy.

Patient education:

* Importance of completing the full course of antibiotics.
* Need for follow-up appointments.

Lifestyle modifications:

* Encourage patients to maintain a healthy lifestyle, including regular exercise and balanced diet.

### 6. Contraindications and Precautions

Contraindications:

* Hypersensitivity to colloids or crystalloids
* Severe renal impairment (creatinine > 2 mg/dL)

Special populations:

* Elderly: use caution when administering fluids and medications.
* Pregnancy: use caution when administering medications, especially those with potential teratogenic effects.

Drug interactions:

* Monitor for potential interactions between colloids/crystalloids and other medications.

Warning signs requiring escalation:

* Decreased urine output (< 0.5 mL/kg/hour)
* Increased creatinine (> 2 mg/dL)
* Hypotension (systolic BP < 90 mmHg)

### 7. Key Clinical Pearls

Important clinical tips:

* Early recognition and treatment of sepsis are critical to preventing organ dysfunction.
* Use caution when administering fluids and medications in special populations.

Common pitfalls to avoid:

* Delayed recognition of sepsis, leading to organ dysfunction.
* Inadequate fluid replacement, leading to hypovolemia.

Red flags:

* Decreased urine output
* Increased creatinine
* Hypotension
